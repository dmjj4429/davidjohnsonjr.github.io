01 — About

Who I Am

I'm David Johnson Jr. — a Systems and Network Administrator, active-duty U.S. Marine, and founder of Advantage Studios. My work sits at the crossroads of technology, operations, and creative production.

My background spans IT infrastructure, AV production, military logistics, and team leadership. Whether managing secure systems, supporting enterprise logistics, or building a live event setup, I bring precision, adaptability, and discipline.

I hold a B.S. in Game Design, an M.S. in Entertainment Business, and I am completing my M.S. in Business Intelligence at Full Sail University. Those programs shaped how I think: creative, strategic, technical, and data-driven.

Outside of work, I am a dedicated father and someone who believes consistency beats random bursts of motivation. I train, build, lead, and keep progressing.

Business Intelligence · Completed
Foundations of Business Intelligence and Analytics

This class introduced the bigger picture of Business Intelligence and helped me understand how organizations use data to make smarter decisions. Before moving deeper into databases, ETL, analytics tools, and enterprise data systems, this course gave me the foundation for why BI matters in the first place.

The biggest lesson was that Business Intelligence is not just about charts or reports. It is about turning raw data into useful insight that leaders can actually use. That connects directly to my experience in IT and military logistics because accurate reporting, accountability, and visibility are what allow leadership to make informed decisions under pressure.

This course also helped me see how BI connects business strategy, technology, and communication. A company can collect large amounts of information, but if that information is not organized, analyzed, and presented clearly, it will not support good decision-making. That idea applies to both large enterprise systems and my long-term plans for Advantage Studios.

By the end of the course, I had a clearer understanding of BI as a professional discipline. It gave me the language and structure to explain how data supports operations, strategy, forecasting, and performance improvement across an organization.

BI FoundationsAnalyticsDecision-MakingData Strategy
Takeaway: Business Intelligence turns information into direction when the data is structured, analyzed, and communicated clearly.
Business Intelligence · Active Course
Enterprise Data Management

This month has reinforced that data by itself is not valuable unless an organization has a structured way to manage it. Enterprise Data Management is less about simply storing information and more about building systems that allow data to move reliably between departments, platforms, and decision-makers.

A major focus has been understanding how enterprise systems connect together through ETL pipelines, data warehouses, analytical databases, cloud platforms, and governance models. Before this course, I mostly viewed data from an operational perspective because of my background in IT systems and military logistics. Now I am beginning to think about data architecturally: how it flows, where it breaks, how it scales, and why structure matters before analytics can even begin.

One of the strongest connections I made was between Enterprise Data Management and the work I already do inside the Marine Corps. Systems like GCSS-MC depend entirely on accurate data input, accountability, and standardized processes. If records are wrong, reports become unreliable, equipment tracking breaks down, and leadership loses visibility into operational readiness.

The cloud analytics portion of the course also helped me better understand why organizations compare platforms like AWS, Azure, Google Cloud, and Snowflake based on cost, performance, flexibility, and ecosystem fit. These decisions are not just technical choices. They affect how quickly a business can report, forecast, scale, and respond to change.

This course connects directly to my long-term goals in systems administration, business intelligence, and Advantage Studios. If I want to build scalable operations, I cannot wait until the business grows to care about data structure. Good systems need to be designed early.

Enterprise DataETLData WarehousingCloud Platforms
Takeaway: Enterprise systems only become intelligent when the data structure underneath them is reliable.
Entertainment Business · Completed
Leadership & Management Style

This course helped me define what kind of leader I want to be. Studying different leadership models made me realize that leadership is not just about authority or position. It is about consistency, trust, communication, and the ability to guide people through pressure without losing sight of the mission.

The biggest connection for me was between leadership in the Marine Corps and leadership in a creative business. Both environments require accountability, but people respond better when they understand the purpose behind the work. That lesson applies directly to Advantage Studios because creative teams need structure without feeling controlled.

Takeaway: Leadership works best when discipline and trust operate together.
Entertainment Business · Completed
Finance for Entertainment Business

This course made the business feel real in numbers. Building pro forma income statements, estimating revenue, tracking expenses, and thinking through funding options helped me understand what it actually takes to operate a creative company. It also made me more aware of how quickly poor financial planning can damage a strong idea.

For Advantage Studios, this course helped me think through startup costs, equipment depreciation, payroll, package pricing, and investor expectations. It taught me that financial clarity is not just an accounting task. It is a leadership responsibility.

Takeaway: You cannot pitch what you cannot quantify.
Entertainment Business · Completed
Digital Marketing Strategy

This course connected creative marketing with real analytics. Instead of thinking about promotion as just posting content, I learned how digital strategy uses target markets, keywords, platforms, budgets, KPIs, and campaign goals to create measurable results.

The work directly supported Advantage Studios because it helped me define who the company serves and how to reach those audiences. I also started thinking more seriously about tools like Google Analytics, SEMrush, and Meta Business Suite as systems for decision-making rather than just marketing add-ons.

Takeaway: Marketing without data is just noise.
Entertainment Business · Completed
Artist & Product Management

This course changed how I look at management in the entertainment industry. A manager is not just someone who handles schedules. They guide careers, protect brands, support revenue growth, coordinate teams, and help talent make better long-term decisions.

I connected the course to Advantage Studios by thinking about clients like artists, brands, and creative products that need structure to grow. Intake processes, content calendars, deliverables, budgets, and follow-ups all became part of a larger management system.

Takeaway: Creative talent needs structure if it is going to scale.
Entertainment Business · Completed
Project & Team Management

This course gave me a stronger operational framework for building Advantage Studios. Defining project scope, creating work breakdown structures, identifying risks, building budgets, and managing team roles helped me see how a business idea becomes an actual operating system.

The most useful part was learning how to break a large goal into smaller pieces. Instead of trying to build everything at once, the course showed me how to organize work into phases, assign responsibility, and create systems that reduce confusion before it becomes a problem.

Takeaway: Structure is what separates a business from a hobby.
Entertainment Business · Completed
Entertainment Business Capstone

The capstone pulled the entire Entertainment Business program together. I developed an investor-ready business plan for Advantage Studios that included market research, target audience analysis, competitive positioning, revenue planning, operations, and financial projections.

Presenting the plan forced me to explain not only what the business does, but why it can work and how it can sustain itself. That process helped me identify gaps, sharpen the model, and treat the business plan as a living strategy document instead of a one-time assignment.

Takeaway: A strong business plan is not paperwork. It is a decision-making tool.
