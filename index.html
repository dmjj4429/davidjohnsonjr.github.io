Who I Am

I'm David Johnson Jr. — a Systems and Network Administrator, active-duty U.S. Marine, and the founder of Advantage Studios. I've spent years working at the crossroads of technology, operations, and creative production, and that unique combination is what drives everything I build.

My background spans IT infrastructure, AV production, military logistics, and team leadership — environments that demand precision, adaptability, and the ability to execute under pressure. 04 — Mastery Journal

Academic Reflections

A course-by-course record of what I learned, how I applied it, and why it matters — across every class in my M.S. Entertainment Business and M.S. Business Intelligence programs at Full Sail University. This is not a transcript. It's a thinking record.

M.S. Entertainment Business
Full Sail University  ·  Graduated January 2026
Program Reflection

Going through the entire EBMS program really helped me connect the creative side of entertainment with the business and strategy that keeps it sustainable. Each course built on the last — from leadership and negotiation to finance, marketing, and legal fundamentals. Instead of just learning concepts, I was consistently asked to explain why certain choices made sense, which sharpened my critical thinking and forced me to approach problems from a more strategic, big-picture perspective.

Creating pro forma financial statements, developing full business plans, and building brand and marketing strategies made the learning feel real. I gained a much stronger understanding of how entertainment branding, publishing, distribution, and digital marketing all work together — as well as how legal considerations impact creative and business decisions. I leave the program with more confidence in my leadership skills, a better grasp of financial and operational planning, and a framework for approaching new opportunities with intention and structure.

Entertainment Business
Leadership & Management Style

This course changed how I understand leadership at a fundamental level. Working through Maxwell and Greene as contrasting frameworks forced me to get specific about who I actually am as a leader — not who I think I should be. I landed at 90% Maxwell, 10% Greene. Maxwell's model fits a creative studio environment where trust, collaboration, and authentic loyalty are what keep teams together. Greene's strategic thinking has its place, but in an industry built on passion, people see through cold manipulation fast.

What stuck with me most was Maxwell's point that you have to lead yourself before you can lead anyone else. I brought that directly into how I manage my days — the Marine Corps already taught me discipline, but this gave it a philosophical backbone I could articulate to a team.

Takeaway: Authentic leadership compounds over time. Strategy without character has a ceiling.
Entertainment Business
12-Month Roadmap — Launching a Creative Studio

This wasn't just an assignment — it was the first time I put a real, structured timeline on Advantage Studios. Months 1–2: join industry associations and build a creative community. Months 3–4: expand my professional learning network through LinkedIn, forums, and events. Months 5–6: lock in the legal structure (EIN, C-Corp or LLC registration, licenses). Months 7–10: research and secure funding. Months 11–12: identify location, establish branding, and launch.

What this exercise gave me was specificity. The idea of "one day building a studio" became a sequenced plan with real milestones. I referenced Foote's The Business Side of Creativity throughout and found it grounded every theoretical step in practical decision-making. The Full Sail Alumni Network also came up as a resource I hadn't been fully leveraging — something I changed immediately after this course.

Takeaway: A vision without a timeline is just a wish. Structure is what makes dreams operational.
Entertainment Business
Finance for Entertainment Business

This was the course that made the business feel real in numbers. I built a full Pro Forma Income Statement for Advantage Studios — estimating revenue, tracking expenses, calculating payroll taxes and benefits, and factoring in equipment depreciation. I also worked through loan structures and interest projections, which gave me a much more realistic picture of what outside funding actually costs over time.

The biggest lightbulb moment was understanding how to structure investor equity so it doesn't function like a monthly expense eating into cash flow. That distinction matters enormously when you're building a company that needs to stay lean early. By the end, I had a working financial model I can actually reference and update — not a textbook exercise, but a living document for the business.

Takeaway: You can't pitch what you can't quantify. Financial clarity is a leadership skill, not just an accounting one.
Entertainment Business
Digital Marketing Strategy

I came into this course wanting a real, usable marketing plan for Advantage Studios — not theory to regurgitate. I got exactly that. The biggest shift was understanding integrated marketing: combining social content with local advertising and in-person networking creates a much stronger brand presence than any single channel alone.

Learning to use Google Analytics, SEMrush, and Meta Business Suite connected the creative side of marketing with the analytical side — something I'd been ignoring. The target market analysis section was equally valuable. I knew my audience was a mix of corporate clients and independent creatives, but tools like Nielsen segmentation showed me how to break that down with actual data. The course ended with a complete campaign plan — goals, budgets, content calendar, and KPIs — that I can launch for real.

Takeaway: Marketing without data is just noise. Know your audience before you spend a dollar reaching them.
Entertainment Business
Artist & Product Management

This course reframed what management means entirely. A manager isn't a behind-the-scenes helper — they're the CEO/COO of an artist's career, responsible for contracts, budgets, brand positioning, and long-term trajectory. The shift toward thinking long-term rather than chasing one-off wins was the most valuable part of this for me.

I applied everything directly to Advantage Studios. Client intake became artist onboarding — clear expectations, calendar planning, and a few key metrics (leads, conversions, package value). I built a 90-day content plan to demonstrate proof of work and reliability. Even multi-room AV event budgeting started to feel like tour planning: crew, gear, logistics, contingencies. Negotiation also improved — I started setting baselines, documenting terms, and making follow-ups clean and consistent. That alone protected margins.

Takeaway: Revenue must be diverse and consistent. Mastery looks like tight calendars, reliable deliverables, and predictable income streams.
Entertainment Business
Media Publishing & Distribution

My goal entering this course was to understand how content moves from creation to audience — and more importantly, how creators protect ownership and revenue along the way. The course delivered. Working through case studies involving T.J. Everett and Waverly Keane made the legal and business principles concrete, not theoretical. I learned how contracts, PROs (performance rights organizations), and publishing splits actually function in the industry.

The distinction between physical and digital distribution, and the role of platforms like FilmHub and TuneCore for independent creators, was directly applicable to what I'm building. The most valuable insight: successful distribution isn't just about creativity — it's about understanding ownership, negotiation, and long-term revenue planning from day one. For Advantage Studios, this means structuring licensing agreements properly and designing distribution plans that retain ownership of the work we produce.

Takeaway: Know who owns what before you create it. Distribution strategy begins at the contract, not the release.
Entertainment Business
Project & Team Management

This course gave me a real operational framework for Advantage Audio Visual. Week by week, the concepts built on each other in a way that felt directly applicable rather than academic.

Week 1 — Project Definition: Slowing down to define purpose, goals, scope, constraints, and stakeholders made the business feel solid instead of something floating in my head. It also clarified what success looks like once the project is fully built out.

Week 2 — Work Breakdown Structure: Breaking the business into smaller sections completely changed how I think about workload. Instead of trying to grow everything at once, the WBS showed me what depends on what, what comes first, and how every step connects. A giant project became a manageable set of tasks.

Week 3 — Risk Management & Budgeting: Looking at the business from a realistic angle — equipment issues, staffing problems, transportation delays, AV chaos — and building actual risk plans made me feel prepared instead of reactive. The budgeting section made startup costs, asset depreciation, staffing projections, and event pricing feel manageable rather than overwhelming.

Week 4 — Team & Quality Management: Team structure and communication matter enormously in AV where timing is everything. Assigning roles, setting expectations, and building accountability keeps the operation smooth. Quality management showed me how to deliver consistent results across multiple events — and how to close out a project properly by reviewing what worked and fixing what didn't.

Takeaway: Structure is what separates a business from a hobby. A good project manager doesn't react — they build systems that prevent the need to.
Entertainment Business
Program Capstone — Business Plan

The capstone was the integration point for everything the program taught. I developed a complete, investor-ready business plan for Advantage Studios — covering market analysis, target audience segmentation, competitive landscape, revenue model, go-to-market strategy, operational structure, and a full financial projection. This wasn't a hypothetical exercise. It was the actual document I intend to use.

Presenting it forced me to articulate not just what Advantage Studios does, but why it's positioned to win in the market and how it sustains itself financially. The process of building it revealed gaps I hadn't fully addressed — pricing strategy for bundled services, the timeline to profitability, and how to structure equity for future partners. Closing those gaps made the plan stronger and the business more real.

Takeaway: A business plan is not a document you write once. It's a thinking tool you return to every time the market shifts.
M.S. Business Intelligence
Full Sail University  ·  In Progress — 2025–2026
Program Reflection — Ongoing

The Business Intelligence program is where the analytical layer gets added to everything I've already built. After completing three degrees in creative and business disciplines, this program is teaching me to quantify, model, and optimize — to take the decisions I've been making intuitively and ground them in data structures, statistical models, and visual reporting systems. Every course builds a skill that plugs directly into how I run Advantage Studios and how I operate in my DoD role.

This journal will grow as the program progresses. Each entry reflects what I actually learned, not what the syllabus said I would.

Business Intelligence
Data Warehousing & Business Intelligence

This was the foundation course and it delivered exactly that — a solid structural understanding of how enterprise data environments are built. ETL pipelines, dimensional modeling, star and snowflake schemas: these aren't abstract concepts anymore. I built a working prototype from raw CSV data and transformed it into a structured, BI-ready warehouse environment with clean, queryable tables.

What surprised me most was how much this mirrors the logistics work I do in GCSS-MC. Both are fundamentally about making sure data goes in correctly, gets stored accurately, and comes out in a usable form for decision-makers. The difference is scale and tooling — the principles are the same. That connection made the material click faster than I expected.

Takeaway: Good data architecture is invisible when it works and catastrophic when it doesn't. Build it right the first time.
Business Intelligence
Python for Data Analysis

Python has been the most immediately applicable course in the program. pandas, NumPy, matplotlib, scikit-learn — these tools take processes I've been doing manually in spreadsheets and make them reproducible, scalable, and fast. Working through data wrangling and exploratory analysis showed me how much insight is sitting in unstructured datasets that most organizations never extract.

I've started applying these concepts directly to Advantage Studios — building scripts that analyze event booking patterns, calculate margin by service type, and flag anomalies in budget projections. The shift from manual analysis to scripted, repeatable analysis is significant. It's the difference between knowing something and being able to prove it with data every time.

Takeaway: Automation isn't about replacing thinking — it's about freeing up thinking for decisions that actually require it.
Business Intelligence
Database Management & SQL

SQL is the language of data — and this course made me fluent enough to work across real relational databases with confidence. Advanced querying, schema design, indexing, stored procedures, and performance optimization are not beginner skills. Learning them in context — applying them to realistic datasets rather than toy examples — made the difference between understanding syntax and understanding when and why to use each tool.

My DoD work involves systems that run on structured databases, and being able to think through how data is organized on the back end has already improved how I query information for reporting and troubleshooting. The ability to write a stored procedure that pulls a LOGSTAT-equivalent report from a structured dataset is directly transferable.

Takeaway: SQL isn't a niche skill — it's a core literacy for anyone who works with organizational data at any level.
Business Intelligence
Data Visualization & Dashboards

This course bridged the gap between having data and actually communicating it. Tableau, Power BI, and Python-based visualization libraries all approach the same problem differently — and knowing when to use which tool is as important as knowing how to use them. The emphasis on communicating complex findings to non-technical audiences is something most data programs undervalue. I don't.

In the military and in business, the person making the decision is rarely the person who built the analysis. That gap — between the analyst and the decision-maker — is where dashboards live. A well-designed dashboard eliminates ambiguity, surfaces the right metrics, and makes action obvious. A poorly designed one buries the insight under noise. This course taught me to tell the difference and build accordingly.

Takeaway: Data that can't be understood can't be acted on. Visualization is not decoration — it's the final mile of analysis.
